3 Banach Energy Space Framework

This chapter formalizes the abstract operator factorization theory from [1]. The derivative \(D = \delta ^*\) is constructed (not axiomatized) via the Banach dual pairing \(\langle DF, u \rangle = \mathbb {E}[F \cdot \delta (u)]\), and its properties—linearity, vanishing on constants, kernel characterization—are all proved from this single identity. The main results are: Theorem A (fluctuation factorization on \(\mathrm{im}(\delta )\)), the representability obstruction (centered even functionals cannot lie in \(\mathrm{im}(\delta _L)\), proved by the even-annihilation/self-pairing argument), Theorem B (the product rule with jump defect \(\Gamma \)), and Theorem C (chaos kernel characterization of \(\ker (D_L)\)). The chapter concludes by instantiating the abstract framework for symmetric \(\gamma \)-stable processes and verifying backward compatibility with the Hilbert formalization via the Hilbert bridge.

3.1 Abstract Framework

The Banach energy space axiomatizes the minimal structure—divergence, duality pairings, and expectation—needed to develop the operator theory without assuming a Hilbert space identification.

3.3 Fluctuation Factorization (Theorem A)

Every centered functional is shown to be representable as a divergence, providing the factorization that the derivative \(D\) inverts.

3.4 Representability Obstruction

The even-annihilation argument shows that centered, even, nonzero functionals cannot lie in \(\mathrm{im}(\delta _L)\), providing the abstract engine for the main non-representability theorem.

Theorem 55 Even annihilates \(\mathrm{im}(\delta )\)
✓
#

Uses

  • Definition 54
  • Theorem 48

Lean declarations

  • BanachEnergySpace.even_annihilates_im_delta

If \(G\) is even, then \(\mathbb {E}[G \cdot \delta (u)] = 0\) for all \(u\). This vanishing is the mechanism by which distributional symmetry produces the obstruction.

Proof ▶

Under \(L \mapsto -L\): \(G\) is invariant, \(\delta (u)\) flips sign. So \(\mathbb {E}[G \cdot \delta (u)] = \mathbb {E}[G \cdot (-\delta (u))] = -\mathbb {E}[G \cdot \delta (u)]\), forcing the value to \(0\). Uses holder_neg and sym.neg_delta.

Theorem 56 Representability obstruction
✓
#

Uses

  • Theorem 55
  • Theorem 46

Lean declarations

  • BanachEnergySpace.representability_obstruction

Proposition 5.1. Centered, even, nonzero \(G \Rightarrow G \notin \mathrm{im}(\delta _L)\). This is the abstract engine of the main theorem [1, Proposition 5.1]: three checkable properties yield non-representability.

Proof ▶

By contradiction: assume \(G = \delta (u)\). Then \(\| G\| _2^2 = \mathbb {E}[G \cdot G] = \mathbb {E}[G \cdot \delta (u)] = 0\) by even annihilation. So \(G = 0\), contradicting \(G \neq 0\).

3.5 Leibniz Defect (Theorem B)

The Leibniz defect \(\Gamma (F,G)\) measures the failure of the product rule for \(D\), which is shown to be entirely determined by the jump structure of the driving process.

Definition 57 Leibniz defect
✓
#

Uses

  • Definition 47

\(\Gamma (F,G) := D(FG) - F \cdot DG - G \cdot DF \in H_X^*\): measures failure of the product rule. \(\Gamma = 0\) iff \(D\) is a derivation, which holds precisely when the driving process has no jumps.

Theorem 58 Product rule with jump defect
✓
#

Uses

  • Definition 57

Lean declarations

  • BanachEnergySpace.product_rule_with_jump_defect

Theorem B. \(\langle \Gamma (F,G), u \rangle = \mathbb {E}[\sum _s \Delta _s(f,g) \cdot \delta (u)]\). The defect is entirely determined by the jump structure of the process [1, Theorem B].

Proof ▶

Apply the Itô formula to \(fg\), multiply by \(\delta (u)\), take expectations. The Leibniz part cancels, leaving the jump residual \(\sum _s \Delta _s(f,g)\). Uses product_ito_paired_derived.

Theorem 59 Defect vanishes without jumps
✓
#

Uses

  • Definition 57

Lean declarations

  • BanachEnergySpace.defect_vanishes_without_jumps

No jumps \(\Rightarrow \) \(\Gamma = 0\): the classical product rule for continuous processes. This recovers the Hilbert setting as a special case, confirming consistency with the companion formalization.

Proof ▶

If \(\sum _s \Delta _s(f,g) = 0\), the product rule identity reduces to \(\langle \Gamma , u \rangle = 0\) for all \(u\), giving \(\Gamma = 0\) by nondegeneracy.

3.6 Chaos Characterization (Theorem C)

The kernel of \(D_L\) is characterized as the space of functionals whose chaos projections vanish, showing that even kernels and higher chaoses make \(\ker (D_L)\) infinite-dimensional.

Theorem 60 Chaos kernel characterization
✓
#

Uses

  • Theorem 48

Lean declarations

  • BanachEnergySpace.chaos_kernel_characterization

Theorem C. \(D_L F = 0\) on deterministic integrands iff \(\Phi _M \to 0\) as \(M \to \infty \). Even kernels and higher chaoses satisfy the vanishing condition, making \(\ker (D_L)\) infinite-dimensional. This characterization shows that the kernel of \(D_L\) captures precisely the chaos-orthogonal functionals [1, Theorem C]; cf. [9, 10] for the Poisson chaos expansion.

Proof ▶

The \(L^2\) isometry reduces \(\mathbb {E}[F \cdot \delta _L^M(u)] = \int u \cdot \Phi _M\, ds\). Truncation convergence passes \(M \to \infty \). The conclusion follows from nondegeneracy of the \(L^q\)-\(L^p\) pairing.
